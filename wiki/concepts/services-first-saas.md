# Services-first SaaS

The sequencing thesis from [[greg-isenberg|Isenberg]]'s [[isenberg-future-of-saas-30-step|playbook]] (Steps 11–14): start as a human-fulfilled service, then automate it into agent-fulfilled software once you've documented every step and learned which steps are mechanical vs. judgment.

## The core argument

> "A lot of these future of SaaS businesses are actually going to start off as service businesses with human beings at the core of it. It's actually going to start as being you the person to actually go and fulfill the task."

Why:

1. **You can't automate what you don't understand.** Doing the workflow manually exposes the steps, the edge cases, the judgment calls. Sketching the workflow in a doc misses both.
2. **You learn the customer's actual willingness to pay** before sinking dev time. If they won't pay you to do it, they won't pay an agent to do it either.
3. **You build initial revenue and case studies** during the build phase, not after launch. Step 27 (case studies) and Step 28 (hiring operators from inside the niche) become possible because you started as the operator.

## The sequence

```
Step 11: Manually perform the workflow.
Step 12: Document every step precisely.
Step 13: Separate judgment from mechanical tasks.
Step 14: Turn mechanical tasks into agent workflows.
Step 15: Design agents to complete full tasks.
```

Steps 11–13 are pre-product. Steps 14–15 are where the SaaS starts. Most founders try to compress 11–13 into "I'll prototype the agent and figure it out as I go." Isenberg's claim is the prototype-first founders consistently miss judgment edges that the manual-first founders catch.

## How this differs from "consulting that becomes a product"

Both start with services. The difference is intent:

- **Consulting → product:** sells custom expertise; the product extracts a fragment of it.
- **Services-first SaaS:** sells the *exact* workflow that will become the product, at *exactly* the deliverable shape it'll ship as. The service is the alpha.

[[productized-services]] addresses this from the productization side. Services-first SaaS is the same idea from the *time direction*: start at the human end of the productization spectrum, walk it toward the automated end as the workflow proves out.

## Tension with Finn's existing rhythm

Overlook is already a services business. Productizing an Overlook workflow into agent-SaaS is therefore *more* aligned than building a new SaaS from scratch — Finn already has the customers, the deliverable shape, the price points. The candidate workflows in Finn's surface are listed in [[agent-native-saas]].

The risk is that running a services-first SaaS during the agency phase splits attention. Mitigated by:

1. Picking a workflow Overlook *already* runs for paying clients (so the service revenue is already there)
2. Pricing the manual phase higher than the agent phase will be (so each automation step improves margin)
3. Limiting the manual phase to ≤3 customers before automating, to keep the time commitment bounded

## Three free-distribution wedges into the SaaS layer

Services-first is one of three free-distribution channels into the SaaS layer Isenberg has surfaced across his 2026 episodes. Each lowers the customer-acquisition cost for the SaaS by earning audience attention through a non-SaaS surface first:

1. **Services-first** — earn customers by being the human operator. Direct revenue, slow scale. The original framing in this concept.
2. **[[media-first-distribution|Media-first]]** — earn customers by running a content engine in the niche. Indirect revenue, fast brand. Steps 6–10 of the 30-step playbook.
3. **SEO-as-distribution via directories** — earn customers by ranking for their searches. Both direct (AdSense / affiliate at the directory layer) and indirect (SaaS upsell to listed businesses) revenue. New in 2026-05 — [[isenberg-frey-chu-directory-2025|Isenberg × Frey Chu]] surfaces it as a parallel wedge to services-first; tactical recipe in [[profitable-directory-pattern]].

The three are composable. A services-first SaaS founder running a media engine and a directory captures three distribution surfaces for one SaaS. Each is a different work mode (operator, content engine, SEO build), but each is also a different audience entry point.
