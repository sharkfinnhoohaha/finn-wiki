# Media-first distribution

The thesis from [[greg-isenberg|Isenberg]]'s [[isenberg-future-of-saas-30-step|playbook]] (Steps 6–10, reinforced at Steps 21 and 27): for a niche [[agent-native-saas]], the content engine *is* part of the product, not a marketing afterthought. The founder builds an audience and a SaaS in parallel, with each feeding the other.

## The core argument

> "You're going to not just want to create a product, my friends. No, no, no, no, no. You're going to want to create media."

Three reasons it matters:

1. **Audience precedes ads.** Organic content tells you which messages convert in this specific subniche. Once an organic angle works, it becomes an ad — same script, paid distribution.
2. **Media is the moat against bigger competitors.** A venture-backed SaaS in the same space can outspend you on ads but can't manufacture five years of niche-specific content overnight.
3. **The content captures emails** (Step 10). When ads stop working and organic decays, the email list is what you have left.

## The five-step content sub-loop

Compressed from Steps 6–10:

```
6. Create scroll-stopping content for the workflow.
7. Study what gets saves, replies, DMs.
8. Double down on organic angles that convert.
9. Run paid ads on proven organic winners.
10. Capture emails from day one.
```

The cadence Isenberg specifies elsewhere in the episode: **one piece minimum every single day.** Pick one channel (IG / TikTok / X). Use AI as the CMO — research viral patterns in the niche, generate non-obvious ideas, build a calendar. Daily Schedule Tasks for content ideation.

## Why "AI CMO" is more than a slogan

Isenberg names the agentic content workflow specifically: use [[claude-code]] / [[openclaw|Cowork]] / Manus / ChatGPT to:

- Research how others go viral *in this niche* (not generic content advice)
- Generate non-obvious content ideas, not the obvious ones
- Build a content calendar
- Generate scripts
- Generate (or outline) the actual content
- Run on Schedule Tasks so it produces ideas daily without prompting

This is the [[wat-framework-marketing|WAT framework]] applied to founder-led content. The same agent-orchestration substrate that runs the SaaS workflow also runs the content engine.

## Contrast with current Overlook posture

[[overlook-strategy]] does not currently run a media engine. The brand has [[overlook-strategy-positioning|positioning]], a portfolio site, and [[client-portal-as-differentiator|the client portal]] as differentiator — but no recurring public content stream. The closest is Finn's individual posting on [[identity-and-brand-architecture|the personal brand]] surface, which is intentionally light.

Two ways to apply media-first distribution if Finn pursued an [[agent-native-saas]] line:

- **Build an audience for the SaaS line, not the agency.** Pick a niche-specific channel handle (e.g., for an aviation-school workflow tool: `@cfimaintenance` or similar). Treat it as a distinct brand from Overlook. The SaaS has its own audience, the agency has clients.
- **Use the agency itself as the content surface.** Vibe-coded sites, branding case studies, agent-built portfolio launches. The audience that watches an Overlook content engine is the audience that hires Overlook. This is closer to [[vibe-coding|the lead-with-vibe-coding option]] from the positioning fork.

These are mutually compatible but require different daily content commitments.

## What "scroll-stopping" actually means in 2026

Isenberg gives one concrete metric: 60–80–120 likes counts as viral *within a niche.* Don't benchmark against Mr. Beast. Benchmark against the median post in the same subniche tag and aim 5–10× above it.

His own example: ~2,700 likes / ~5,000 bookmarks on a recent Instagram post is what he treats as a hit and what gets recycled into ads. The bookmark count matters more than likes — saves indicate intent to refer back, which is the highest-value signal.
